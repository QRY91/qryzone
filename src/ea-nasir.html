The oldest complaint tablet

The copper was bad in 1750 BC. Nothing is new.

March 2026

The clay is still there. The British Museum has it.

Nanni pressed his grievance into wet clay roughly 3,775 years ago: the copper was bad, Ea-nasir refused to fix it, the messenger was treated with contempt. He wanted his money back. He wanted an apology. He wanted it on record.

The record survived. Nanni didn't. Ea-nasir didn't. The copper definitely didn't.

This is the oldest known written complaint. A one-star review. A contractor dispute. The only reason we know about it is that someone, in the middle of being wronged, sat down and wrote it down.

Ea-nasir had multiple complaint tablets. This wasn't a one-off. Customers kept coming because he had supply chains. The copper stayed bad. He never apologized. We know all of this because people wrote it down.

The writing is why it survived. The writing is the point.

Every generation of builders rediscovers this. Contractor disputes. Governance failure. The vendor who overpromises and underdelivers and keeps the contract anyway. Not because the patterns are unknowable — they're documented, they have names, the reading exists. But reading history feels less urgent than moving fast. The clay tablet is a long-term project. The copper needs to ship.

Ea-nasir's complaint tablets are held by the British Museum. The most famous, from Nanni, dates to approximately 1750 BC and is considered the oldest known written customer complaint.
