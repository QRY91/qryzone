Who This Is For

Finding your people by making content you'd want to read

"Make content you would want to consume, for people who share your curiosity"

Stop trying to figure out what "the audience" wants. Make the thing you want to exist. The people who share your interests will find it.

The Kind of Developer This Resonates With

Not everyone. That's fine. You're not looking for everyone.

  • Curious about how, not just what: Wants to see the debugging session, not just the finished code
  • Values depth: Will read the long post if it's actually interesting
  • Building their own tools: Productivity hacking, workflow optimization, scratching their own itches
  • Local-first curious: Interested in reducing external dependencies
  • AI as tool, not magic: Uses AI practically, skeptical of hype
  • Solo/indie path: Thinking about working independently, or already doing it

If you're nodding at most of these, we probably have things to talk about.

Two Sites, Two Purposes

Here's the trick: instead of diluting yourself for different audiences, make different spaces for different contexts.

Professional site

  • Business-focused portfolio
  • For employers, clients, LinkedIn
  • Professional but honest
  • Real projects with real impact
  • No compromise on authenticity, just framing

Personal site

  • Uncompromising personal expression
  • For fellow curious developers
  • Completely authentic - informal, nerdy, raw
  • Messy process, experiments, rabbit holes
  • Zero compromise. This is your space.

The benefit: you can be fully authentic in both. Professional site doesn't have to be boring. Personal site doesn't have to worry about "what will employers think."

Discovery works both ways. Some people find the professional site first and get curious about the person behind it. Others find the weird personal stuff and realize you might be good to work with.

The Actual Strategy

Not a marketing strategy. A "find your people" strategy.

  1. Make what you want to exist: Don't guess at market demand. Follow curiosity.
  2. Share consistently: Let your natural audience find you over time.
  3. Pay attention to who resonates: Not everyone will. That's the filter working.
  4. Double down on genuine connection: The right 10 people beat 1000 passive followers.
  5. Resist dilution: Don't compromise for broader appeal. That's how you lose what makes you interesting.

What Makes It Work

  • Authenticity attracts alignment: Real content finds people who actually care
  • Curiosity creates community: Shared learning interests beat professional networking
  • Process beats products: How you work is often more interesting than what you built
  • Separation enables honesty: Different spaces for different contexts
  • Sustainability comes from interest: You can't fake enthusiasm forever

The bet: Making exactly what you want to make, consistently, finds better collaborators than trying to appeal to everyone.

The evidence so far supports this.
