QRY Zone: Living Workshop

What if portfolios showed process instead of polish?

Traditional portfolios are graveyards of finished work. Here's what I shipped. Look how clean. Next.

But that's not how development actually works. The interesting part happens in the middle - debugging at 2am, refactoring something three times, realizing your architecture was wrong and starting over.

QRY Zone is an experiment: what if the portfolio was the workshop? Willy Wonka's factory with philosophical undertones, but for software.

What a Living Workshop Shows

  • The messy middle: Debugging logs. Refactoring commits. Ideas that didn't work.
  • Honest timelines: Not "built this in a weekend" when it took three months.
  • Tools you can poke: Live demos. Source code. Actual databases.
  • Connecting patterns: How one project leads to the next.

Building in public means showing the "unknown unknowns" - the stuff you figure out that isn't in any tutorial. That's where developer connection actually happens.

Ideas Worth Stealing

The Public Ideas Database

Ship an actual SQLite file with your site. Let people browse your project ideas, half-baked concepts, and tool notes. The tagline:

"Ideas are cheap. Take however many you want. If you build it before me, show me!"

This does two things: demonstrates that execution matters more than ideation, and invites collaboration from people who might build something interesting.

Visual Status Indicators

  • Neon signs: "QRY is DEBUGGING" / "QRY is SHIPPING"
  • Real build status from CI/CD
  • Terminal-style navigation
  • Zenburn colors everywhere (developer-familiar)

Interactive Workspace

PS1/PicoCAD style 3D diorama of your physical dev setup. Click on the keyboard, see the firmware repo. Click on the monitor, see current project. Click on the coffee cup, get a 404.

Keeping It Human

AI helps write content. That's fine. But the workshop needs to feel inhabited.

What makes it feel real:

  1. Acknowledge where AI helped (don't pretend you wrote everything)
  2. Leave in the informal language - slang, humor, lowercase
  3. Show failures. The stuff that broke.
  4. Let things be imperfect. Perfect is suspicious.

Design Philosophy

Start minimal. Add things only when they serve a purpose. Each element should create contrast with what was there before.

The site evolves like software. That's the point. Static portfolios die. Living workshops change as you do.

Technical approach:

  • Static site generation (fast, simple)
  • SQLite in the repo (transparent data)
  • GitHub Actions for status updates
  • Progressive enhancement (works without JS)

Why This Might Work

Process is more interesting than product. Most portfolios show what you built. Few show how you think.

Transparency builds trust. Not the marketing kind. The "I can see your git history and your half-finished ideas and you're clearly a real person" kind.

Sites that change attract people who like watching things grow. That's a good filter for the kind of collaborators you actually want.

The bet: Showing the messy work creates better connections than showing the polished results.

We'll see if it works.
